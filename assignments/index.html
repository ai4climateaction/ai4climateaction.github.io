Assignments and Evaluation Criteria

Reflections

Each week during the forked lessons, you will submit one paper reflection based on one of that week's required readings. Reflections are graded on a complete/incomplete basis and should be up to one page in length.

A good reflection goes beyond summarizing the paper. Begin with a short summary (1-2 sentences) to orient the reader, then focus the majority of your reflection on your own critical analysis. In particular, your reflection should address:

  • How the paper frames the context and motivates the underlying problem
  • The methodology proposed and whether it is well-suited to the problem
  • What you find convincing and what you are less persuaded by, and what you might have done differently
  • Possible extensions of the work — think beyond "apply a newer ML method" and reflect on the underlying problem itself

If you are looking for guidance on how to read and engage with research papers, the following resources may be helpful:

  • How to Read Journal Articles (MIT CommLab)
  • How to Read a Paper (Harvard CS 261)

Presentations

Each group will have 15 minutes to present, with up to 25 minutes total allocated per group to allow time for questions and discussion. Your presentation should cover five components: a summary of the application and its broader context, a summary of the method, a critique, proposed extensions, and discussion questions for the class.

Rubric (100 points)

Summary of application and its broader context (25 points)

Present a mature framing of the "gap" within the problem domain, including a connection to the climate impact of filling that gap. Frame the "methodological gap" and how it connects to the problem gap. Establish common ground for your audience — introduce key technical terms and concepts clearly. Think about what you did not understand when you first started reading, and remember that your audience is your peers.

Summary of the method (25 points)

Describe the method accurately and in sufficient detail. Contextualize it within prior literature: spell out the differences from what was done before and explain what is significant about the proposed approach.

Critique (25 points)

Provide an in-depth analysis of what was done well and what was done poorly, considering the perspectives of problem motivation, problem framing, methods development, and evaluation.

Extensions (10 points)

Propose 1-2 extensions that are not trivial (e.g., simply swapping in a new architecture). Extensions should reflect a holistic, nuanced view of the problem and method — for example, new problem owners, new methodological directions, or new application contexts.

Food for thought / questions for discussion (15 points)

Prepare 3 discussion questions for the class that provoke thoughtful conversation about the paper and its broader implications.
